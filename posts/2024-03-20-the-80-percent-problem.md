---
title: "The 80% Problem"
date: 2024-03-20
description: "Getting to 80% is easy. The remaining 20% is where the real work happens — and where most things stop."
tags: [engineering, product, craft]
---

There's a pattern I keep seeing.

A feature gets built to 80%. It mostly works. The happy path is clean. Edge cases are known but deferred. The team ships it, moves on, and the last 20% becomes permanent.

Over time, the product becomes a collection of 80% things, and the whole feels oddly unfinished even when it looks complete.

## Why 80% is sticky

The first 80% is exciting. You're building something new. Progress is fast and visible.

The last 20% is different. It's edge cases. Error states. The thing that only happens on certain screen sizes, or when the user does something unexpected, or when the API returns a shape you didn't plan for.

It's unglamorous. It doesn't fit in a demo. It's hard to estimate. It doesn't get celebrated.

So it doesn't happen.

## The compounding cost

A single 80% feature is fine. Two is manageable. Ten starts to feel like something is wrong, but you can't point to exactly what.

What's wrong is trust. Users learn — unconsciously, through small frictions — that the product is a little unreliable. They work around it. They lower their expectations. They stop trying new features because they assume the edges will be rough.

Trust is hard to rebuild.

## What to do

**Name the 20% explicitly.** When you ship, write down what you didn't do. Not as a backlog item — as a known limitation. Make it visible.

**Finish things before starting things.** This is hard organizationally, but the math works out.

**Let the 20% determine the timeline, not the other way around.** If you can't finish it, make the scope smaller. An 80% version of a smaller thing is still 80%.

The goal isn't perfection. It's not shipping things you're quietly embarrassed about.

There's a difference.
